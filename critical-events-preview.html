Asystole

Perioperative cardiac arrest with a flat, non-shockable rhythm and no pulse · asystolic arrest · non-shockable arrest (with PEA)

Premium
When to suspect
  • Abrupt loss of the EtCO2 waveform and arterial line pulsation
  • Flat line on the ECG with no palpable central pulse
  • Preceding severe bradycardia, often after a vagal surgical stimulus
  • Plummeting SpO2 or a suddenly non-tracing pulse oximeter
  • Unmeasurable or absent blood pressure (late)
  • Fixed, dilated pupils (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Announce the arrest, call for help and the code cart, and assign a manual reader.
  2. Confirm no pulse, then start high-quality CPR at 100-120/min, depth 2-2.4 in (at least 5 cm), full recoil.
  3. Stop all volatile agents and any infusion that deepens hypotension.
  4. Switch to 100% oxygen high flow and hand-ventilate at about 10 breaths/min; do not over-ventilate, and confirm the tube, circuit, and chest rise.
  5. Attach defibrillator pads to confirm the rhythm; asystole and PEA are not shockable.
  6. Give epinephrine 1 mg IV as soon as feasible, then repeat every 3-5 minutes.
  7. Check rhythm and pulse every 2 minutes for no more than 10 seconds; rotate compressors.
  8. Confirm asystole in a second lead and verify leads and gain before calling it a flat line.
  9. Hunt and treat reversible causes (Hs and Ts) in parallel, weighted to the operative context.
  10. Use intra-arrest TTE or TEE to help identify the cause, and consider ECMO where available with a reversible cause.

Pitfalls
  • Calling asystole on a loose lead, low gain, or fine VF; verify in a second lead.
  • Delaying compressions to chase a diagnosis; CPR and cause-hunting run together.
  • Forgetting that anesthesia arrests are usually a treatable cause, not primary cardiac.
  • Overlooking dynamic hyperinflation; disconnect the circuit to let auto-PEEP escape.
  • Slamming full-dose epinephrine in suspected LAST instead of titrated microgram boluses.
  • Missing occult hemorrhage, high spinal, or a mainstem tube as the true driver.

After ROSC, transfer to ICU with continued monitoring, 12-lead ECG, labs, and targeted temperature management as indicated; treat the identified cause definitively. Unstable Bradycardia

Pulse present with heart rate too slow (typically <50 bpm) to sustain adequate perfusion · symptomatic bradycardia, hemodynamically unstable bradyarrhythmia

Premium
When to suspect
  • Falling heart rate on the monitor, usually below 50 bpm, that does not respond to lightening stimulation
  • New hypotension, narrowing pulse, or a dropping end-tidal CO₂ tracing accompanying the slow rate
  • Signs the bradycardia is causing poor perfusion: shock, ischemic chest discomfort, or acute heart failure
  • In an awake patient: acutely altered mental status, lightheadedness, or syncope
  • A recent trigger — intense vagal stimulus (peritoneal traction, laryngoscopy, ocular pressure), hypoxia, or a rate-slowing drug (beta blocker, calcium channel blocker, dexmedetomidine, remifentanil, digoxin, neostigmine)
  • Progression to junctional rhythm, high-grade AV block, ventricular escape, then asystole (late)
  • Loss of pulse — convert to the asystole/PEA arrest pathway (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Confirm a pulse; if absent, start CPR and switch to the asystole/PEA algorithm.
  2. Call for help and bring the code cart and defibrillator/pacer to the room.
  3. Give 100% oxygen at high flow and confirm adequate ventilation and oxygenation.
  4. Remove the vagal trigger now: tell the surgeon to stop traction, release ocular or carotid pressure, desufflate the abdomen, and drain a distended bladder.
  5. Reduce or pause volatile agent, propofol, dexmedetomidine, or opioid infusions that may be slowing the rate.
  6. Confirm working IV or IO access.
  7. Give atropine 0.5–1 mg IV; repeat as needed to a maximum of 3 mg.
  8. If atropine fails, start transcutaneous pacing (set the rate to at least 80 bpm and increase current until capture) and/or an epinephrine or dopamine infusion titrated to perfusion.
  9. Hunt reversible causes: hypoxia, hyperkalemia and other electrolyte disturbance, myocardial ischemia, and drug effect (obtain a 12-lead ECG, ABG, electrolytes, troponin).

Pitfalls
  • Treating the number, not the patient — an asymptomatic, well-perfusing slow rate (e.g., an athlete or a beta-blocked patient) needs observation, not atropine.
  • Relying on atropine in high-grade AV block — it often fails in Mobitz II and third-degree block, so move quickly to pacing and infusions.
  • Giving atropine to a heart-transplant recipient — the denervated heart will not respond; use isoproterenol, epinephrine, or pacing.
  • Under-dosing atropine — give a full 0.5–1 mg dose; very small (sub-therapeutic) doses can paradoxically worsen bradycardia.
  • Missing the surgical or vagal cause — peritoneal traction, oculocardiac reflex, and pneumoperitoneum are fixed by stopping the stimulus, not by drugs alone.
  • Overlooking hyperkalemia and hypoxia — a slow, widening rhythm may be the first sign; check a gas and potassium early.
  • Forgetting to capture — transcutaneous pacing requires confirming both electrical and mechanical (pulse) capture and providing analgesia/sedation for the awake patient.
After the crisis

Once the rate and perfusion recover, keep the patient on continuous ECG, blood-pressure, and oximetry monitoring and leave pacer pads in place until stable. Send or repeat a 12-lead ECG, ABG, electrolytes, and troponin to pin down the cause, and consult cardiology and/or the ICU for high-grade block, ongoing infusion needs, or transvenous or permanent pacing. Reconcile the medication list for culprit rate-slowing drugs, document the trigger and response, complete institutional event reporting, and debrief the team. Management above follows the AHA/ACLS 2020 adult bradycardia algorithm and is cross-checked against the Stanford Emergency Manual.

Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA)

Organized cardiac electrical activity on the monitor with no palpable pulse or perfusing output · pulseless rhythm that is not VF/VT/asystole · formerly electromechanical dissociation (EMD)

Premium
When to suspect
  • Loss of pulse or arterial waveform while an organized rhythm persists on the ECG
  • Abrupt drop of the plethysmograph and end-tidal CO2 with an intact tracing
  • Any monitored rhythm without a pulse that is not VF, pulseless VT, or asystole
  • Unresponsive patient, absent central pulse on manual check (late)
  • Agonal or absent respirations under sedation or general anesthesia (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call it out, summon help and the code cart or defibrillator, and note the time.
  2. Start high-quality chest compressions: 100 to 120 per minute, depth 5 to 6 cm, full recoil, minimize pauses.
  3. Confirm the airway and ventilate with 100 percent oxygen; if intubated, give asynchronous breaths about every 6 seconds.
  4. Give epinephrine 1 mg IV or IO as early as possible, then repeat every 3 to 5 minutes.
  5. Recognize PEA is NOT shockable; do not defibrillate. Continue CPR and reassess rhythm every 2 minutes.
  6. Work the reversible causes in parallel (Hs and Ts); attach capnography and use point-of-care ultrasound if available.
  7. Perioperative sweep: stop volatile agent and any suspect drug, confirm circuit and oxygen supply, rule out auto-PEEP by disconnecting the circuit, check for high block, LAST, anaphylaxis, and surgical bleeding.
  8. Watch for ROSC: an abrupt sustained rise in end-tidal CO2 or a return of the arterial waveform.

Pitfalls
  • Treating the monitor, not the patient: an organized tracing without a pulse is arrest, not stability.
  • Giving atropine or trying to defibrillate PEA; neither is indicated and both waste compression time.
  • Running the code without ever finding the cause; PEA rarely resolves until the Hs and Ts are fixed.
  • Missing auto-PEEP in an obstructive or ventilated patient; disconnect the circuit to let air escape.
  • Overlooking OR-specific triggers: tension pneumothorax, gas embolism, LAST, anaphylaxis, high neuraxial block, or occult hemorrhage.
  • Interrupting compressions too long for pulse or rhythm checks and letting perfusion pressure fall.
After the crisis

After ROSC, support airway, oxygenation, and blood pressure; obtain a 12-lead ECG, arterial blood gas, and electrolytes; and continue treating the cause that precipitated the arrest. Target normoxia and normocapnia, avoid hyperthermia, and arrange ICU-level monitoring and targeted temperature management per protocol. Debrief the team, document the event and timeline, and file the required incident and quality report. Ventricular Fibrillation / Pulseless VT

Shockable pulseless arrest rhythm requiring immediate defibrillation and CPR · VF · pVT · shock-refractory arrest

Premium
When to suspect
  • Sudden loss of pulse with a chaotic, wide, or fast waveform on the monitor
  • Abrupt loss of the arterial line tracing and pulse oximeter plethysmograph
  • Sharp drop or loss of end-tidal CO2 despite ventilation
  • Unresponsive patient who is not breathing normally, intraoperatively or in PACU
  • Witnessed collapse with no palpable central pulse (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help, the code cart, and a defibrillator; note the time.
  2. Start high-quality chest compressions: 100 to 120 per minute, at least 2 inches (5 cm) deep, full recoil.
  3. Confirm the rhythm is shockable and defibrillate without delay.
  4. Resume compressions immediately after the shock; do not pause to recheck a pulse.
  5. Establish IV or IO access and secure the airway; ventilate with 100% oxygen, roughly 10 breaths per minute, and do not overventilate.
  6. After the second shock, give epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO and repeat every 3 to 5 minutes.
  7. Reanalyze the rhythm every 2 minutes; shock again if still VF/pVT, and it is reasonable to step up the energy on later shocks.
  8. For a rhythm that stays shockable, give amiodarone 300 mg IV/IO push or lidocaine 100 mg IV/IO push.
  9. Search for and correct reversible causes (H's and T's).

Pitfalls
  • Delaying the first shock to place lines, intubate, or give drugs — defibrillation is the definitive therapy.
  • Long pauses in compressions around shocks and rhythm checks lowering the compression fraction.
  • Escalating too slowly: use the highest safe or manufacturer-recommended energy for repeat shocks.
  • Missing fine VF and misreading it as asystole; confirm leads, gain, and pad contact.
  • Treating a shockable rhythm as PEA/asystole (no shock) or shocking a truly non-shockable rhythm.
  • Forgetting reversible triggers common in the OR: hypoxia, hyperkalemia, local anesthetic toxicity, tension pneumothorax, hypothermia.
After the crisis

After ROSC, begin post-cardiac-arrest care: support oxygenation and blood pressure, obtain a 12-lead ECG, treat the precipitating cause, and consider targeted temperature management and coronary evaluation. Transfer to an ICU with continuous monitoring. Debrief the team, document the timeline, drugs, and shocks, and file the required event report. Scope cross-checked against the Stanford Emergency Manual.

Stable Tachycardia (SVT with a Pulse)

Sustained rate >150/min with a pulse and no serious instability signs · PSVT, AVNRT, AVRT, narrow-complex tachycardia

Premium
When to suspect
  • Abrupt-onset regular narrow-complex tachycardia, often >150/min, with a palpable pulse
  • No P waves or retrograde P waves; rate too fast and fixed for sinus tachycardia
  • Patient is perfusing: adequate blood pressure, mentating, no ischemic chest pain
  • Rules out a treatable driver first: pain, light anesthesia, hypovolemia, fever, hypoxia, hypercarbia
  • Hypotension, altered mentation, cool/mottled shock, chest pain, or acute pulmonary edema (late) — this is now UNSTABLE, cardiovert
Immediate actions
  1. Confirm a pulse and that the patient is stable; reassess perfusion continuously.
  2. Call for help and a defibrillator; place pads or paddles in case of decompensation.
  3. Give supplemental oxygen, secure IV access, and attach continuous ECG, SpO2, and BP.
  4. Capture a 12-lead ECG and a rhythm strip; classify QRS as narrow (<0.12 s) or wide, regular or irregular.
  5. Rule out and treat reversible drivers (pain, light plane, hypovolemia, hypoxia, fever).
  6. For narrow and regular: attempt vagal maneuvers (Valsalva or carotid sinus massage) first.
  7. If vagal fails, give adenosine 6 mg rapid IV push with a fast saline flush; escalate to 12 mg.
  8. If it persists or converts to atrial fibrillation/flutter, rate-control with diltiazem or a beta-blocker.
  9. For wide-complex or any diagnostic doubt, get expert consultation before antiarrhythmics.

Pitfalls
  • Treating sinus tachycardia as SVT — chase the cause (pain, hypovolemia, light anesthesia), do not push adenosine.
  • Slow or non-flushed adenosine: its half-life is seconds, so a sluggish push simply fails.
  • Giving adenosine or a nodal blocker for irregular wide-complex (pre-excited atrial fibrillation/WPW) can accelerate to VF.
  • Assuming a wide-complex tachycardia is SVT — treat as VT until proven otherwise; get help.
  • Stacking a calcium-channel blocker and a beta-blocker risks profound bradycardia and hypotension.
  • Anchoring on "stable" — if hypotension, ischemia, or altered mentation appear, switch to the unstable pathway and cardiovert.
After the crisis

Keep continuous ECG, SpO2, and cyclic BP; obtain a post-conversion 12-lead to look at baseline rhythm and pre-excitation. Send electrolytes (potassium, magnesium) and correct deficits, and screen for triggers (light anesthesia, hypovolemia, hyperthyroidism, stimulants). Document the rhythm, interventions, doses, and response; escalate to cardiology for recurrent or wide-complex arrhythmia. Distinct from the unstable SVT card, whose definitive treatment is synchronized cardioversion. Scope cross-checked against the Stanford Emergency Manual; management follows the AHA ACLS 2020 adult tachycardia (with a pulse) algorithm.

Unstable Tachycardia

A rapid rhythm (usually >150/min) causing hemodynamic compromise · unstable SVT · rapid AF/flutter with a pulse · unstable VT with a pulse

Premium
When to suspect
  • Sustained heart rate typically above 150/min on the monitor
  • New hypotension that tracks with the fast rate
  • Ischemic chest pain or fresh ST changes
  • Acute dyspnea, rales, or new pulmonary edema (acute heart failure)
  • Altered mental status or diaphoresis (late)
  • Cool, mottled skin and poor perfusion (shock) (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help and the code cart; inform the team and assign a recorder.
  2. Confirm a pulse first; if pulseless, switch to the arrest (PEA) algorithm.
  3. Call it unstable for any of: a sudden or continuing sharp drop in blood pressure, acute ischemia, or SBP under 75.
  4. Increase to 100% oxygen at high flow, confirm ventilation, and under anesthesia turn the volatile agent down.
  5. Apply pads, secure IV access, and attach continuous monitoring.
  6. If unstable, move straight to immediate synchronized cardioversion; do not wait on drugs.
  7. Sedate the conscious patient first if it will not delay the shock.
  8. Confirm the defibrillator is in SYNC mode before every shock.
  9. Read the width and regularity to pick the starting energy.
  10. While prepping to shock (do not delay), for regular narrow-complex give adenosine 6 mg rapid IV push via the vein closest to the heart.
  11. If a shock fails, re-arm SYNC and step the energy up for the next synchronized attempt; reassess rhythm and perfusion each time.

Pitfalls
  • Chasing drugs while the patient decompensates; instability means shock now.
  • Delivering an unsynchronized shock by leaving SYNC off; sync must be reconfirmed after each attempt.
  • Starting too low for irregular narrow-complex; rapid AF needs 120–200 J.
  • Leaving SYNC on for a wide, irregular rhythm; it needs unsynchronized defibrillation at 200 J.
  • Giving adenosine for irregular or wide-irregular rhythms, risking deterioration.
  • Blaming the rate for hypotension when sepsis, hypovolemia, or fever is driving a sinus tachycardia; cardioversion will not fix that.
  • Under-sedating an awake patient when a brief delay would have been safe.
After the crisis

Once converted, keep the patient on the monitor, obtain a 12-lead ECG, and recheck electrolytes, oxygenation, and any triggering illness. Document the rhythm, energy delivered, sedation, and response. Escalate to cardiology or intensive care for recurrent or refractory rhythms and arrange a clear post-event disposition. Debrief the team and, if the case occurred under anesthesia, complete the anesthesia record and any required event report. Perioperative Myocardial Ischemia

Oxygen supply/demand mismatch injuring myocardium during anesthesia · type 2 MI, demand ischemia, MINS

Premium
When to suspect
  • New ST-segment depression or elevation on the monitor (lead II + V5)
  • New arrhythmia — onset of tachycardia, ectopy, or heart block
  • Unexplained hypotension or a rising filling pressure
  • Setting of demand stress: tachycardia, hypertension, anemia, hypoxemia, light anesthesia
  • New regional wall-motion abnormality on TEE (late)
  • Rising troponin or overt pump failure / pulmonary edema (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help, get the code cart, and inform the team of a possible cardiac event.
  2. If hypoxemic, increase to 100% O2 at high flow and confirm adequate ventilation.
  3. Verify the ischemia — expand the monitored leads and obtain a 12-lead ECG.
  4. Treat hypotension or hypertension to rebalance coronary supply against demand; support diastolic BP so coronary perfusion pressure is protected.
  5. Be ready for arrhythmias: keep the code cart at the bedside and consider applying pads.
  6. Give a beta-blocker to slow the heart rate; hold it for bradycardia or hypotension.
  7. Discuss aspirin with the surgeon before giving it — weigh surgical bleeding risk.
  8. Call the STEMI team or cardiology stat; decide heparin ± clopidogrel jointly with cardiology and surgery.
  9. Treat pain with a narcotic (fentanyl or morphine).
  10. Consider a nitroglycerin infusion, held until any hypotension is treated.
  11. Place an arterial line and send labs: ABG, CBC, troponin.
  12. If anemic, transfuse packed red cells to restore oxygen-carrying capacity.
  13. Consider TTE or TEE to follow volume status and regional wall motion; consider central venous access.
  14. If hemodynamically unstable, consider an intra-aortic balloon pump.

Perioperative Hypotension

A sudden or sustained fall in arterial blood pressure during anaesthesia that threatens organ perfusion — worked through the physiologic differential (MAP = CO × SVR: decreased preload, low SVR, decreased contractility, low rate) to find and treat the cause · intraoperative hypotension, anaesthesia-related hypotension, shock under anaesthesia

Premium
When to suspect
  • Any low or falling arterial pressure — a common working threshold is MAP below ~65 mmHg or a drop greater than ~20–25% from baseline, sustained rather than a single artefactual reading
  • Confirm it is real first: recycle the NIBP, check the arterial-line transducer height, zero, and waveform, and correlate with a palpable pulse, plethysmograph, and end-tidal CO₂ before treating a number
  • Rising heart rate with a narrow or thready pulse, or a falling end-tidal CO₂ from reduced cardiac output
  • Decreased-preload clues: brisk blood/irrigation loss or concealed haemorrhage, hypovolaemia, high airway pressures with air-trapping (auto-PEEP), new arrhythmia, IVC compression from positioning, embolism (air, clot, fat, amniotic), pneumothorax, pericardial tamponade, venodilators, pneumoperitoneum, prone or head-up posture
  • Low-SVR clues: recent neuraxial or spinal block, induction agent or bolus of vasodilator, warm flushed periphery, urticaria or bronchospasm (anaphylaxis), fever with vasodilation (sepsis), neurogenic shock, endocrine abnormality
  • Decreased-contractility clues: ischaemic ECG changes, deep anaesthetic or a myocardial depressant just given, known low ejection fraction, valvular disease, hypoxaemia, or symptoms after a regional block (local anaesthetic toxicity, LAST)
  • Low-rate clues: bradycardia, including a vagal stimulus
  • Rapidly lethal causes to rule out at once: occult haemorrhage, vasodilators, auto-PEEP, tension pneumothorax, anaphylaxis, a cardiac event (myocardial ischaemia, low ejection fraction, systolic anterior motion of the mitral valve, hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy), pneumoperitoneum or surgical manipulation, and IVC compression
  • Falling or lost end-tidal CO₂ trace, mottled skin, oliguria, ST changes, or rising lactate = failing perfusion approaching arrest (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Confirm the reading is real — recycle the cuff, check the arterial-line transducer level, zero and waveform, and correlate with pulse, pleth, and end-tidal CO₂.
  2. Give 100% oxygen at high flow and confirm ventilation and a good end-tidal CO₂ trace.
  3. Call for help early, tell the surgeon, and consider pausing the procedure; get the code cart if perfusion is poor or falling.
  4. Check the pulse and rhythm — if pulseless or a malignant rhythm, go straight to the ACLS pathway (asystole/PEA, bradycardia, VT/VF).
  5. Reduce anaesthetic depth — turn the volatile and IV agents down or off, since anaesthetic overdose and vasodilation are among the most common and most reversible causes.
  6. Restore preload — confirm a working large-bore IV or IO, run a rapid crystalloid or colloid bolus, and raise the legs or tilt head-down.
  7. Support pressure now while you diagnose — give a vasopressor bolus to temporize: phenylephrine 50–100 mcg IV or ephedrine 5–10 mg IV, repeated and titrated to response (Stanford names both agents but leaves the bolus dose to the provider).
  8. Escalate for severe or refractory hypotension — epinephrine 10–100 mcg IV and/or vasopressin 1–4 units IV, then start an infusion (adjunct: epinephrine or norepinephrine) rather than chasing with repeat boluses.
  9. Inspect the surgical field — look for visible or concealed haemorrhage, pressure or retraction on the heart or great vessels, insufflation pressure, or caval compression, and have these relieved.
  10. Find and treat the cause using the physiologic differential (MAP = CO × SVR) — sort into decreased preload, low SVR, decreased contractility, or low rate; anaphylaxis, tension pneumothorax, tamponade, embolism, auto-PEEP (disconnect the circuit to check), and LAST each have their own specific rescue.
  11. Use point-of-care echo (TEE or TTE) when available to sort volume status, ventricular and valvular function, and outflow obstruction.

Pitfalls
  • Treating the number before the patient — always confirm with a recycled cuff, a properly levelled and zeroed transducer, and a pulse/pleth/EtCO₂ correlate before chasing an artefact.
  • Reaching for a pressor without asking why — a vasopressor buys time, but hypotension has a cause; hunt the physiologic differential (preload, SVR, contractility, rate) in parallel.
  • Missing the rapidly lethal causes — tension pneumothorax, tamponade, embolism, anaphylaxis, auto-PEEP, and LAST kill fast and each need a specific rescue, not just more fluid and phenylephrine.
  • Forgetting auto-PEEP — in an obstructed or bronchospastic patient, air-trapping drops preload; briefly disconnect the circuit to diagnose and decompress.
  • Wrong pressor for the physiology — phenylephrine can worsen a low-output/bradycardic state, while pure inodilators can drop SVR further; match the drug to preload, SVR, contractility, and rate.
  • Anaphylaxis mistaken for “light” or routine hypotension — unexplained collapse with high airway pressures needs epinephrine and fluids, not a deeper plane.
  • Overlooking caval or surgical compression — positioning (prone, obese, gravid), retractors, packing, or insufflation can obstruct venous return; look at and communicate with the field.
  • Under-resuscitating haemorrhage — pressors mask ongoing bleeding; find the source and give blood products rather than crystalloid alone.
  • Chasing with repeated boluses — if you are re-dosing a pressor, start an infusion and escalate; refractory hypotension is a red flag to broaden the differential.
After the crisis

Once pressure is restored, keep the patient in a monitored setting and continue any pressor infusion, weaning as tolerance allows; establish additional large-bore or central access and an arterial line if not already in place. Send an arterial blood gas with haemoglobin, lactate, glucose, calcium, and potassium, place a urinary catheter, and track urine output as a perfusion marker. Reassess with point-of-care echo to confirm the working diagnosis, and if hypotension is persistent despite maximal therapy escalate to mechanical support (ECMO or bypass) and involve critical care and the relevant specialty early. Document the timeline, the presumed cause, and every drug and dose; disposition (PACU vs ICU) follows the severity, the cause, and the response. Where the hypotension pointed to a specific event — anaphylaxis, LAST, transfusion reaction, embolism — follow that event’s reporting and follow-up pathway, and complete institutional incident reporting and a team debrief. Perioperative Hypoxemia

A fall in arterial oxygen saturation during anesthesia · low SpO2 · desaturation

Premium
When to suspect
  • Falling pulse-oximeter reading with a clean plethysmograph waveform
  • Rising peak inspiratory pressure or stiffer bag on hand ventilation
  • New wheeze, absent breath sounds, or an asymmetric chest
  • Sudden drop or loss of the ETCO2 tracing (circuit or airway problem)
  • Tachycardia, then bradycardia and dysrhythmia (late)
  • Central cyanosis (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Deliver 100% oxygen at high fresh-gas flow.
  2. Read the gas analyzer to rule out a low delivered FiO2 or high nitrous oxide; if either is present, treat it as an oxygen-supply failure.
  3. Check the other vitals and feel a pulse; confirm the SpO2 waveform is real, not artifact.
  4. Look for a capnograph trace—its loss suggests a disconnect, extubation, or low output.
  5. Switch to hand ventilation to feel lung compliance and rule out a leak or machine fault.
  6. Auscultate both lungs and confirm tube position, then pass a suction catheter to clear secretions and check for obstruction.
  7. Consider pneumothorax, and call for the code cart if the desaturation is severe.
  8. Call for help early and work the differential by category while treating.

  • Chasing the number before confirming FiO2 and a true SpO2 waveform.
  • Trusting the ventilator when hand ventilation would reveal poor compliance or a leak.
  • Missing endobronchial intubation after repositioning, flexing the neck, or moving the patient.
  • Normal SpO2 despite methemoglobinemia—the oximeter reads falsely near 85%.
  • Forgetting tension pneumothorax when pressures rise and one lung is silent.
  • Overlooking a mucus plug or blood clot that a suction catheter would clear.
After the crisis

Keep FiO2 high until saturation recovers, then wean while watching the waveform and blood gases. Get a chest film to confirm tube depth and lung expansion and to rule out pneumothorax or aspiration. Document the trigger, the cause found, and the response; hand off the working diagnosis and any imaging or co-oximetry pending. If methemoglobinemia or malignant hyperthermia is confirmed, follow the dedicated pathway and notify the team. Scope and values cross-checked against the Stanford Emergency Manual.

Amniotic Fluid Embolism

Catastrophic anaphylactoid response to fetal antigen during labor, delivery, or cesarean — abrupt collapse, hypoxemia, and DIC · AFE · anaphylactoid syndrome of pregnancy

Premium
When to suspect
  • Sudden agitation, anxiety, or "sense of impending doom" in a laboring or just-delivered patient
  • Abrupt dyspnea and falling SpO₂ (<90%) without another explanation
  • Precipitous hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg) or bradycardia at or just after delivery
  • Seizure activity (occurs in a large minority of cases)
  • Cardiovascular collapse or cardiac arrest around delivery (late)
  • Torrential hemorrhage with uterine atony and overt DIC (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help — activate the code and rapid-response teams; summon OB, anesthesia, and neonatology.
  2. Place the patient in left uterine displacement and, if arrest occurs, start high-quality CPR while keeping displacement continuous.
  3. Establish large-volume IV access, favoring the upper body; add central access or an IO line in the humerus when able.
  4. Increase to 100% oxygen at high flow and prepare for emergent intubation.
  5. Give ACLS epinephrine and defibrillate shockable rhythms per standard algorithm.
  6. Support the circulation with IV fluid, vasopressors, and inotropes for shock and RV failure (see table).
  7. Anticipate cardiopulmonary arrest and emergent cesarean; if maternal arrest occurs with a viable fetus, deliver without delay.
  8. When feasible, place an arterial line for continuous pressure monitoring.
  9. Anticipate massive hemorrhage and DIC: send a stat coagulation panel, fibrinogen, and type & crossmatch, and follow the massive transfusion guideline.
  10. Consider mechanical circulatory support (IABP, ECMO, or cardiopulmonary bypass) for refractory collapse.

Debrief the team, document the timeline, and report the case to the AFE Registry (afesupport.org / Baylor). Scope cross-checked against the Stanford Emergency Manual.

Perioperative Anaphylaxis

Rapid-onset systemic hypersensitivity to an agent given under anaesthesia, causing cardiovascular collapse, bronchospasm, and/or angioedema — often without skin signs · anaesthetic anaphylaxis, intraoperative anaphylactic shock, perioperative hypersensitivity (grade III–IV)

Premium
When to suspect
  • Sudden, unexplained hypotension or cardiovascular collapse after induction or any new agent — the most common and often the first sign under anaesthesia
  • Rising airway pressures, bronchospasm, or difficulty ventilating
  • Tachycardia (occasionally bradycardia) with weak or absent pulse, out of proportion to blood loss or anaesthetic depth
  • Rapid desaturation not explained by a ventilation problem
  • Flushing, urticaria, or angioedema — helpful when present, but frequently absent or hidden under drapes; do not wait for them
  • Timing clue: onset within minutes of IV induction agents, NMBA, or antibiotic; chlorhexidine and latex may appear later (10–60+ min) as surgery proceeds
  • Falling EtCO₂ progressing to loss of trace = impending arrest (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help; declare “anaphylaxis” and summon senior anaesthesia help plus the crash cart / anaphylaxis kit.
  2. Stop the likely trigger — halt the IV antibiotic, colloid, chlorhexidine prep, latex exposure, or any drug just given; remove suspect items from the field.
  3. Give high-flow 100% oxygen, secure the airway, and confirm ventilation; hand-ventilate if airway pressures are high.
  4. Turn off or minimize volatile and other vasodilating agents once amnesia is otherwise ensured; tell the surgeon to pause.
  5. Give epinephrine early — IV if a line is available, titrated to severity (see table); if no IV access, IM epinephrine 0.3–0.5 mg (1 mg/mL) into the anterolateral thigh, repeat q5min.
  6. Bolus IV crystalloid wide open and elevate the legs; large volumes are typical.
  7. If refractory, repeat and escalate epinephrine, then start an infusion; add vasopressin, glucagon (if β-blocked), or norepinephrine as needed.
  8. If cardiac arrest, start CPR / ACLS now with epinephrine 1 mg IV q3–5min and aggressive fluids.
  9. Once stabilized, draw the first timed serum tryptase — do not let it delay resuscitation.

  • Wrong epinephrine dose or concentration — in a perfusing patient give micrograms (10–100 mcg), not the 1 mg arrest dose; confirm 1:10,000 for IV boluses and 1:1000 for IM.

  • Do not re-expose — never re-administer the suspected agent; flag it clearly before any further anaesthetic.

Refer to a drug-allergy / immunology clinic for skin testing (and specific IgE where available) about 4–6 weeks after the event to identify the culprit and safe alternatives before future anaesthesia. Malignant Hyperthermia

A hypermetabolic skeletal-muscle crisis triggered by volatile anesthetics and/or succinylcholine in susceptible patients (usually RYR1/CACNA1S), driven by uncontrolled myoplasmic calcium release · MH crisis, malignant hyperpyrexia

Premium
When to suspect
  • Unexplained rising ETCO2 despite increased minute ventilation — earliest, most sensitive sign
  • Unexplained tachycardia or other dysrhythmias
  • Masseter spasm / trismus after succinylcholine (may herald MH)
  • Generalized muscle rigidity (can occur even after a non-depolarizing relaxant)
  • Tachypnea; CO2 absorbent hot or exhausted early; mixed respiratory and metabolic acidosis on ABG
  • Hyperkalemia, rising CK, cola-colored urine (late)
  • Rapidly rising core temperature (late) — do not wait for it to act
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help, declare an MH emergency, and send a runner for the MH cart and dantrolene.
  2. Stop all triggering agents — discontinue volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine at once.
  3. Hyperventilate with 100% oxygen at fresh-gas flows of at least 10 L/min; increase minute ventilation.
  4. Convert to a non-triggering anesthetic (propofol/TIVA); do not delay dantrolene to swap the circuit or absorbent.
  5. Give dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg IV by rapid push through a large-bore line; repeat until ETCO2, rigidity, and heart rate fall. More than 10 mg/kg may be needed.
  6. Call the MHAUS 24-hour Hotline at 1-800-644-9737 (outside North America +1-209-417-3722) for real-time guidance.
  7. Treat hyperkalemia (calcium, insulin plus glucose, bicarbonate) and send a potassium with the first labs.
  8. Cool actively if core temperature is above 39 °C or rising fast; reassess and repeat cooling measures every ~5 min as needed, and stop at below 38 °C to avoid overshoot.
  9. Treat dysrhythmias with standard agents — avoid calcium channel blockers, which can cause hyperkalemia or arrest with dantrolene.
  10. Support diuresis to more than 1 mL/kg/hr, place a Foley, watch for myoglobinuria, and draw serial labs.

Intraoperative Bronchospasm

Acute reversible small-airway constriction during anesthesia, raising airway pressure and impairing ventilation · perioperative bronchospasm, reactive airway event

Premium
When to suspect
  • Rising peak inspiratory pressure on a volume-controlled breath — often the earliest objective sign in a paralyzed, intubated patient
  • Upsloping "shark-fin" ETCO₂ waveform: slanted phase II, loss of the flat phase III plateau — a sensitive early cue
  • Expiratory wheeze; prolonged expiratory phase and breath-stacking (auto-PEEP)
  • Falling tidal volume in pressure-controlled modes, or a stiff bag on hand-ventilation
  • Recent trigger: airway instrumentation or light anesthesia, endobronchial tube tip, aspiration, a new drug or blood product, asthma/COPD/recent URI, smoker
  • Decreased or absent breath sounds — a "silent chest" means airflow too low to make sound (late)
  • Falling SpO₂ with rising ETCO₂ — gas exchange already failing (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help; announce "bronchospasm — rising airway pressures."
  2. Switch to 100% O₂ at high fresh-gas flow.
  3. Hand-ventilate to feel compliance and confirm the problem is the patient, not the machine.
  4. Rule out mechanical mimics fast: pass a suction catheter down the ETT (mucus plug/kink), confirm tube depth, exclude pneumothorax.
  5. Deepen anesthetic depth: increase volatile agent (sevoflurane/isoflurane) and/or give propofol; use ketamine if hypotensive or refractory.
  6. Give albuterol 8–10 puffs via inline adapter into the circuit/ETT; repeat every 10 min.
  7. Ventilate for air-trapping: slow the rate, prolong expiratory time, accept permissive hypercapnia; watch for auto-PEEP and hypotension.
  8. If severe/refractory or SpO₂ falling below ~80%, give titrated IV epinephrine; escalate to an infusion if needed.
  9. Add adjuncts: magnesium sulfate 2 g IV; hydrocortisone 100 mg IV for the late/recurrent phase.
  10. Treat the cause: withdraw an endobronchial tube, suction aspiration, deepen for light anesthesia. If anaphylaxis is suspected, treat as anaphylaxis (epinephrine first, stop the trigger).

Pitfalls
  • Confirm it's the patient, not the equipment — a kinked ETT, mucus plug, mainstem intubation, cuff herniation, or circuit obstruction mimics bronchospasm. Hand-ventilate and pass a suction catheter first.
  • A "silent chest" is worse, not better — absent breath sounds can mean airflow too low to generate any sound.
  • Don't miss anaphylaxis: bronchospasm with hypotension or rash/angioedema after a drug, latex, chlorhexidine, or blood product means epinephrine first and stop the trigger. Bronchospasm may be the only early sign.
  • Air-trapping causes hypotension — if BP crashes, disconnect the circuit and let the chest fully deflate, then resume with longer expiratory time. Also rule out tension pneumothorax.
  • Steroids are not a rescue drug — hydrocortisone does nothing in the first minutes.
  • Light anesthesia plus airway stimulation is a common trigger — deepening is often the fix; don't reach only for drugs.
  • Wheeze that won't respond to β₂-agonists — reconsider the diagnosis (fixed obstruction/mass, pulmonary edema, aspiration).
After the crisis

Do not extubate into an active bronchospasm; emergence commonly triggers recurrence once volatile is off, so keep albuterol and IV bronchodilators ready. Monitor SpO₂, the ETCO₂ waveform (a returning square shape signals improvement), PIP/compliance, and an ABG if severe; anticipate a biphasic or recurrent course, especially if steroids were given late. Disposition to PACU with close respiratory monitoring, or ICU if refractory, if an epinephrine infusion was needed, or after severe hypoxemia. Document the trigger, timeline, drugs, doses, and response; if a drug, latex, or blood product is implicated, send serial tryptase levels and refer for allergy/immunology work-up and label the chart. Can't Intubate, Can't Oxygenate (CICO)

Oxygenation fails by mask, tracheal tube, and supraglottic airway at once — the final airway pathway that mandates emergency front-of-neck access · CICO · CICV · failed airway

Premium
When to suspect
  • Falling SpO₂ that will not recover despite optimized attempts — this is the defining trigger, not the intubation failure itself.
  • Loss or flat-lining of the capnography waveform — the most objective early sign that no gas is moving.
  • Failed tracheal intubation after optimized attempts (repositioning, video laryngoscopy, best operator, adequate paralysis).
  • Failed face-mask ventilation: no chest rise, no capnography trace, rising resistance despite two-hand technique plus oral and nasal airways.
  • Failed supraglottic airway: no ETCO₂ trace after best-attempt placement of a 2nd-generation SGA.
  • Bradycardia, cyanosis, hemodynamic collapse (late) — do not wait for these to act.
Immediate actions
  1. Declare it out loud: "This is a CICO situation." Assign a leader and a timekeeper.
  2. Call for the difficult-airway cart and cric-capable help now — extra anesthesia plus the most-skilled airway operator or surgeon (ENT); activate your institution's difficult-airway pathway.
  3. Optimize oxygenation on the current device: 100% FiO₂, two-person mask technique, oral and nasal airways, jaw thrust; keep apneic or high-flow nasal oxygen running throughout.
  4. Limit and count attempts. Cap tracheal intubation at 3 (plus 1 more only if a more skilled clinician arrives). Change something each time — blade, operator, adjunct — or move on; never repeat a failing technique.
  5. Confirm paralysis is adequate — inadequate relaxation is a reversible cause of "can't intubate, can't ventilate." Do not rely on succinylcholine wearing off to rescue the patient.
  6. Attempt SGA rescue: insert a 2nd-generation supraglottic airway (i-gel, LMA ProSeal or Supreme). Limit to 3 or fewer insertion attempts; confirm ventilation with capnography.
  7. Decision point: if oxygenation is restored by mask or SGA, stop, oxygenate, and think — wake the patient if feasible, or secure the airway by a controlled route.
  8. If you still cannot oxygenate, declare CICO and go to emergency front-of-neck access now. Open the cric kit and perform a scalpel–bougie–tube cricothyrotomy; do not delay for repeat oral attempts.
  9. Front-of-neck technique: extend the neck, laryngeal handshake to find the cricothyroid membrane, transverse stab through the membrane with the scalpel, rotate the blade, railroad a bougie into the trachea, advance a cuffed 6.0 mm tube over it, inflate the cuff, confirm with capnography, secure.
  10. Confirm and stabilize placement with continuous ETCO₂; hand over to a definitive surgical airway (formal tracheostomy / ENT) as appropriate.

Pitfalls
  • CICO is defined by oxygenation, not intubation. Repeated intubation attempts while SpO₂ falls is the classic fatal trap — an airway you can't secure but can still oxygenate is not CICO.
  • Do not wait for succinylcholine to wear off. Spontaneous ventilation after 1 mg/kg returns far too late to prevent hypoxic injury, and reversal is not a substitute for front-of-neck access once CICO is declared.
  • Falling SpO₂ is a late, lagging number. By the time the oximeter drops steeply, desaturation is already advanced — act on the failed-oxygenation state, not the plummeting number.
  • "Just one more look" kills. Uncounted, repeated laryngoscopy causes trauma and bleeding and delays rescue — assign someone to count and enforce the attempt limit.
  • Don't skip the SGA. A 2nd-generation SGA rescues many failed-mask-plus-failed-intubation cases before front-of-neck access is needed.
  • Locating the cricothyroid membrane is the hard part — use the laryngeal handshake early; a scalpel technique is superior to needle or cannula methods for reliable oxygenation in adults.
  • Task fixation with no leader. Declare the emergency out loud and hand off the airway attempt so a fresh operator can proceed to front-of-neck access.
After the crisis

Disposition to the ICU with a secured, capnography-confirmed airway; arrange a definitive surgical airway (formal tracheostomy) with ENT or surgery. Monitor with continuous ETCO₂ and SpO₂, and watch for hypoxic sequelae (serial neuro checks), aspiration, airway bleeding, subcutaneous emphysema, pneumothorax, and cric-site injury. Run a structured team debrief and document the airway-failure sequence, devices, and attempt count. Issue the patient a difficult-airway alert letter and register the event (MedicAlert or a national difficult-airway registry) so future providers are warned, and consider anesthesia consult-clinic referral. File the institutional critical-incident report per local policy. There is no national CICO hotline — escalation is to your institution's difficult-airway and ENT service. Delayed Emergence

Failure to regain expected consciousness within ~10-15 min of stopping anesthetic · prolonged/failed emergence, unresponsiveness after GA

Premium
When to suspect
  • No purposeful movement or eye opening despite anesthetic off and adequate time elapsed
  • Shallow or absent spontaneous breathing, low respiratory rate suggesting residual opioid
  • Weak grip, floppy tongue, or head-lift failure pointing to residual paralysis
  • Pinpoint pupils (opioid) or fixed/unequal pupils (neurologic)
  • Cool skin, low core temperature, or a long high-anesthetic case
  • Rising end-tidal CO2 or hypoventilation raising concern for CO2 narcosis
  • Low glucose, or a sodium or other electrolyte derangement
  • New focal weakness, asymmetry, or gaze deviation on exam (late)
  • Seizure activity or a prolonged postictal state (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Confirm oxygenation and ventilation first; support the airway and give 100% oxygen.
  2. Stop all anesthetic and sedative infusions; verify vaporizer and lines are truly off.
  3. Check a train-of-four; if block persists, reverse with sugammadex or neostigmine plus glycopyrrolate.
  4. Send a point-of-care glucose, core temperature, ABG, and electrolytes; rule out CO2 narcosis and abnormal sodium.
  5. Warm the patient actively toward normothermia if hypothermic.
  6. Suspect opioid excess? Titrate naloxone 40 mcg IV, repeating every 2 min up to 400 mcg.
  7. Suspect benzodiazepine? Consider flumazenil 0.2 mg IV every minute (max 1 mg), weighing seizure risk.
  8. Suspect scopolamine or anticholinergic delirium? Give physostigmine 1 mg IV with atropine ready.
  9. Examine pupils, symmetry, and reflexes; if focal or stroke suspected, get stat head CT and neurology.
  10. Rule out a medication swap or dosing error as the cause.

Pitfalls
  • Reaching for reversal agents before confirming the patient is oxygenating and ventilating.
  • Missing residual paralysis because you never placed a nerve stimulator.
  • Giving flumazenil reflexively and precipitating seizures in a chronic benzodiazepine user.
  • Over-reversing opioids with naloxone, triggering severe pain, hypertension, or pulmonary edema.
  • Forgetting simple, common causes: hypothermia, hypoglycemia, and hypercarbia.
  • Anchoring on drug effect and delaying imaging when a stroke or seizure is the real cause.
After the crisis

Do not extubate until airway reflexes and strength return; keep the patient monitored in PACU or ICU until reliably awake. Re-sedation can follow naloxone or flumazenil because both are shorter-acting than the drugs they reverse, so watch for a relapse. Document the differential worked through, the labs and TOF findings, and the response to each intervention. If a neurologic cause is confirmed, involve neurology and consider the stroke pathway. Airway Fire

Combustion inside the airway or breathing circuit when an ignition source meets fuel in an oxidizer-rich field · airway/endotracheal tube fire · surgical fire

Premium
When to suspect
  • Flame, flash, or pop at the surgical field during airway/head-and-neck work with laser or electrocautery
  • Sudden smoke, soot, or an acrid/burning odor from the field or circuit
  • Any airway laser or ESU activation while FiO2 is elevated or nitrous oxide is running
  • Unexpected rise in airway pressure, loss of tidal volume, or a melted/charred tube (late)
  • Falling SpO2, wheeze, or stridor after the event (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call it out: announce "airway fire" and mobilize the whole team at once.
  2. Simultaneously: stop ventilation and disconnect the circuit from the anesthesia machine.
  3. Simultaneously: remove the endotracheal tube.
  4. Simultaneously: stop the flow of all airway gases (oxygen and nitrous oxide).
  5. Pour saline or water into the airway to extinguish and cool it.
  6. Remove any burning or flammable material (tube fragments, sponges, drapes) from the field.
  7. Only once the fire is confirmed out, re-establish ventilation; avoid supplemental oxygen (and nitrous oxide) if possible.
  8. Reintubate promptly, before edema develops, coordinated with the surgeon.
  9. Perform rigid bronchoscopy to assess thermal injury and retrieve residual debris.
  10. Inspect the removed tube: confirm no fragment remains in the airway; save all materials.

  • Reflexively ventilating with 100% oxygen after the fire — you are feeding the oxidizer leg of the fire triangle.
  • Leaving oxygen and nitrous oxide running while trying to extinguish — remove fuel AND cut the oxidizer.
  • Delaying tube removal to "assess" — the burning tube is the fuel; pull it first.
  • Running high FiO2 or nitrous oxide during laser/cautery near the airway — the classic setup.
  • Skipping bronchoscopy or the tube inspection — retained fragments and unrecognized thermal injury are missed.
  • Extubating too early after a thermal burn — progressive edema can obstruct hours later.
After the crisis

Admit and monitor for delayed airway edema; keep the airway secured until a leak and safe conditions are confirmed. Assess for inhalation injury, carbon monoxide exposure, and lower-airway burns; obtain chest imaging and involve ENT/critical care. Prevention is the real cure: keep the three legs of the fire triangle apart — minimize the oxidizer (FiO2 <0.30, no nitrous oxide, allow several minutes to wash out before laser use), control ignition (laser only when the team confirms low FiO2), and manage fuel (laser-resistant tube, saline-filled cuff tinted with methylene blue, saline-soaked pledgets, water immediately available). Surgical Fire (Patient / Non-Airway)

A fire on, in, or around the patient outside the airway — drapes, skin prep, or dressings ignite when an oxidizer, fuel, and ignition source meet · OR fire, surgical fire, non-airway fire

Premium
When to suspect
  • A sudden pop, spark, or flash at the surgical field — often the first cue during a high-risk head, neck, or upper-chest case
  • Visible flame on the drapes, skin, prep pooling, or a sponge; a near-invisible pale-blue alcohol flame against blue drapes is easy to miss
  • Smoke, an unexpected burning odor, or unexplained heat under or around the drapes
  • Cautery or laser fired moments earlier in an oxygen-enriched field (open O2 by mask or nasal cannula, MAC head/neck case)
  • A team member calls out fire, or a monitor or drape begins to melt or char
  • Patient movement, agitation, or a pain response under sedation from a developing skin burn
  • Airway involvement (fire tracks toward the tube or circuit) (late) — now also manage as an airway fire
  • Cutaneous or inhalation burn injury (late) — the downstream harm; act before it appears
Immediate actions
  1. Inform the team, call for help, and call for a fire extinguisher; these next steps happen almost simultaneously.
  2. Stop the flow of all airway gases to the patient — turn off oxygen and nitrous oxide and, if needed, disconnect the breathing circuit to remove the oxidizer.
  3. Remove the burning and flammable materials off the patient immediately so another team member can extinguish them.
  4. Extinguish the patient fire: if electrical equipment is burning, use only a CO2 fire extinguisher (safe in wounds); if the fire is non-electrical, extinguish it with saline and soaked gauze.
  5. Care for the patient: ventilate with room air (self-inflating bag), control bleeding, and assess injuries and vital signs — deliberately avoiding supplemental oxygen or nitrous oxide until the fire is confirmed out.
  6. Assess the patient for burns and, if not intubated, for smoke-inhalation injury; inspect the airway if the fire was anywhere near it.
  7. If smoke or fire continues: consider evacuating the patient and the OR per local protocol, close the OR doors, turn off the external gas supply to the room, and alert the fire department.
  8. Once out, examine the field and drapes for smoldering or reignition before proceeding or transporting.
  9. Adjunct (not on the Stanford aid): maintain anesthesia with a propofol infusion or IV agent so the patient does not become light while inhaled agent and oxygen delivery are interrupted.

Reinforce prevention for the next at-risk case — lowest FiO2 for adequate SpO2, avoid N2O, allow alcohol prep to dry, and configure drapes to prevent oxygen buildup. Massive Hemorrhage / Massive Transfusion

Life-threatening blood loss requiring large-volume, balanced component replacement · exsanguination · MTP

Premium
When to suspect
  • Visible large-volume loss, brisk surgical bleeding, or a bleeding cavity
  • Tachycardia and narrowing pulse pressure early
  • Rising transfusion demand: replacing one blood volume within hours
  • Falling hemoglobin despite ongoing resuscitation
  • Rising lactate and worsening base deficit
  • Diffuse oozing from raw surfaces and IV sites signals coagulopathy (late)
  • Frank hypotension refractory to fluid (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help, inform the team, and order the massive transfusion guideline per local protocol; alert the blood bank and name a runner.
  2. Increase to 100% oxygen at high flow and treat hypotension with an IV fluid bolus.
  3. Secure additional large-bore access as needed; consider intraosseous access, and attach a rapid infuser and fluid warmer to every line.
  4. Give vasopressor boluses (ephedrine, phenylephrine, or epinephrine) as a temporizing measure; consider Trendelenburg or leg elevation.
  5. Replace products early: give 1 unit FFP for every 1 unit PRBC, and 1 apheresis platelet unit for every 6 units PRBC; minimize crystalloid.
  6. Send a type and cross; the blood bank issues emergency-release Type O until crossmatched blood is ready.
  7. Accept a low-normal blood pressure until bleeding is controlled (avoid in TBI).
  8. Give tranexamic acid 1 g IV over 10 min, then 1 g over 8 h (adjunct — not on the Stanford aid).
  9. Follow acid–base status with an arterial line and serial blood gas; monitor for hypocalcemia and send coagulation studies.
  10. Maintain normothermia: warm the room, fluids, and patient with forced-air warmers.
  11. Place a Foley catheter and use cell salvage where the case is non-contaminated and non-malignant.
  12. Ask the surgeon whether to page a vascular surgeon and push for definitive surgical or interventional bleeding control.

  • Accepting a low-normal pressure in traumatic brain injury worsens secondary injury.

Local Anesthetic Systemic Toxicity (LAST)

Life-threatening reaction to excessive plasma local-anesthetic levels causing progressive CNS excitation then cardiovascular collapse · LA toxicity, bupivacaine cardiotoxicity

Premium
When to suspect
  • Perioral numbness, metallic taste, tinnitus, or lightheadedness during or shortly after a block (earliest, most sensitive — often the only warning)
  • Agitation, confusion, slurred speech, or a sense of impending doom
  • Muscle twitching progressing to a generalized seizure (classic CNS sign)
  • Any block using a large local-anesthetic dose, a highly vascular site (intercostal, paravertebral, caudal, interscalene), or an unintended intravascular injection
  • Onset is frequently delayed (minutes after injection, occasionally beyond 30 min) and presentation is often atypical — cardiovascular signs can occur without a preceding seizure, especially with bupivacaine
  • Early hypertension and tachycardia, then bradycardia, conduction block, wide QRS, ventricular arrhythmia, hypotension, asystole (late)
  • CNS depression: drowsiness, obtundation, coma, respiratory arrest (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Stop injecting the local anesthetic immediately.
  2. Call for help and get the LAST rescue kit (20% lipid emulsion) to the room now.
  3. Alert the nearest facility with cardiopulmonary bypass / ECMO capability early, before arrest if possible.
  4. Ventilate with 100% oxygen and secure the airway; avoid hypoxia, hypercarbia, and acidosis, which all worsen toxicity.
  5. Suppress seizures with a benzodiazepine (drug of choice); if only propofol is available, give small increments (about 20 mg) and never large-dose propofol.
  6. Give 20% lipid emulsion early — do not wait for arrest if toxicity is progressing.
  7. For arrhythmia, hypotension, or arrest, start ACLS but modify it: use reduced epinephrine (start at 1 mcg/kg or less) and avoid vasopressin, calcium channel blockers, beta blockers, and any further local anesthetic.
  8. Manage refractory arrest with prolonged effort and continued lipid; escalate to cardiopulmonary bypass / ECMO if unresponsive.
  9. Continue the lipid infusion and monitor after stability.

  • Onset can be delayed — keep monitoring after the block; do not leave or discharge the patient early.
  • No seizure does not rule out LAST — cardiovascular collapse can be the first sign, particularly with bupivacaine.

  • Bupivacaine arrest is prolonged — do not abandon resuscitation early; bridge to CPB/ECMO if refractory.

Laryngospasm

Sustained reflex closure of the vocal cords that partially or completely obstructs the upper airway · glottic spasm, laryngeal spasm

Premium
When to suspect
  • Inspiratory stridor — high-pitched crowing on inspiration (partial closure); earliest audible cue
  • Sudden respiratory silence with visible effort but no air movement — complete closure; quieter is worse, not better
  • Tracheal tug with suprasternal and supraclavicular retractions
  • Paradoxical see-saw chest and abdominal movement against a closed glottis
  • Rising airway pressures, no chest rise, flat capnograph on positive-pressure attempts
  • Classic timing: light plane during induction, at LMA/ETT removal, or emergence; triggered by secretions, blood, or airway stimulus
  • Desaturation (late) — a downstream consequence; do not wait for it to act
  • Bradycardia (late) — especially in children; signals hypoxia and impending arrest
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help and tell the surgeon to stop stimulation.
  2. Remove the offending stimulus — suction blood and secretions, stop airway instrumentation.
  3. Apply 100% oxygen at high flow.
  4. Perform a vigorous jaw thrust and hold firm continuous CPAP (about 15–20 cmH2O) via a tight-sealing mask.
  5. Apply Larson's maneuver — firm bilateral pressure in the laryngospasm notch behind the earlobe while pushing the jaw anteriorly.
  6. Deepen anesthesia with propofol 0.25–0.8 mg/kg IV (about 0.5 mg/kg typical) if the patient is stable.
  7. If spasm persists or SpO2 falls, give succinylcholine 0.1–0.5 mg/kg IV (up to 1–2 mg/kg if refractory); with no IV access give 4 mg/kg IM.
  8. Give atropine 0.02 mg/kg IV for bradycardia, especially in children and before any repeat succinylcholine dose.
  9. Ventilate and intubate once the cords relax; secure the airway.
  10. After it breaks, watch closely for negative-pressure pulmonary edema.

Pitfalls
  • Silence is not safety — stridor stopping can mean complete closure; confirm actual ventilation, not a quieter sound.
  • Do not wait for desaturation or bradycardia; those are late signs, and hypoxic bradycardia in a child is a pre-arrest warning.
  • Incomplete treatment that leaves the airway obstructed kills faster than over-sedation — escalate to succinylcholine early when propofol and maneuvers fail.
  • CPAP done wrong worsens it — excessive pressure distends the stomach and pushes the epiglottis down; ensure a good mask seal plus jaw thrust.
  • Extubate awake or deep, not in between — the light emergence plane (stage 2) is peak risk.
  • Negative-pressure pulmonary edema appears minutes to about 1 hour after the spasm breaks — pink frothy sputum, desaturation, bilateral infiltrates; do not blame new hypoxia on residual spasm.
  • Screen for succinylcholine contraindications even in a crisis, especially pseudocholinesterase deficiency.
After the crisis

Continue 100% oxygen and monitor SpO2 and EtCO2; confirm bilateral breath sounds and adequate tidal volumes. Watch for negative-pressure (post-obstructive) pulmonary edema for at least 1–2 hours — treat supportively with oxygen, PEEP/CPAP or positive-pressure ventilation, and short-term reintubation if needed; reserve diuretics for volume overload, and expect resolution in 12–48 hours. Extend PACU observation and consider admission if NPPE, aspiration, or repeated spasm occurred. If succinylcholine was given, monitor for prolonged block, myalgias, transient hyperkalemia, and bradyarrhythmias. Document and debrief the trigger and event per institutional critical-event policy, and reassess the extubation strategy for future cases. Oxygen Supply Failure

Loss or contamination of the oxygen delivered to the machine · pipeline failure · gas crossover

Premium
When to suspect
  • Low oxygen-supply-pressure alarm sounds on the workstation
  • Delivered FiO2 on the gas analyzer reads low despite adequate set flow
  • Oxygen flowmeter collapses or the flow control feels ineffective
  • Nitrous oxide cuts out as the fail-safe device trips
  • Pneumatic ventilator loses drive and bellows fail to fill
  • Falling SpO2 and, later, bradycardia or dysrhythmia (late)
  • Central cyanosis (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Disconnect the patient from the machine and ventilate by hand with a self-inflating (Ambu) bag on room air, or with an Ambu bag or Jackson Rees circuit fed by a separate full oxygen cylinder and regulator.
  2. Never run the bag off the machine's auxiliary oxygen flowmeter—it draws from the same central source that has failed.
  3. Open the machine's backup oxygen E-cylinder (confirm it is not empty) and disconnect the wall pipeline hose to force flow from the tank into the circuit.
  4. Connect a gas-sampling line at the patient to monitor the delivered gases and confirm the patient is receiving oxygen: about 100% on a cylinder, about 21% on room air.
  5. Maintain anesthesia as needed with intravenous agents and turn off the volatile vaporizer.
  6. Call for help, call for the code cart, and inform the team.
  7. Conserve oxygen: reduce fresh-gas flow to the minimum needed and stop any oxygen-driven devices.
  8. Obtain extra backup sources of oxygen for the case.
  9. Once the patient is more stable, page biomedical or bioengineering to help diagnose the machine while you stay focused on the patient; alert OR leadership, the ICU, and the hospital to a possible large-scale oxygen problem.

Tension Pneumothorax (perioperative)

Trapped pleural air under pressure collapsing the lung and compressing the mediastinum, obstructing venous return · tension PTX

Premium
When to suspect
  • Climbing peak inspiratory pressure with falling tidal volumes on a set ventilator
  • Worsening hypoxemia and a dropping SpO2 despite an increased FiO2
  • Unilateral decreased or absent breath sounds, often hyperresonant to percussion
  • Recent risk: central line, high airway pressures/barotrauma, laparoscopic insufflation, thoracic/supraclavicular block, or chest trauma
  • Subcutaneous emphysema (crepitus) over the neck or chest wall
  • Hypotension and tachycardia unresponsive to fluid or vasopressor (late)
  • Distended neck veins and tracheal deviation toward the opposite side (late)
  • Sudden bradycardia or cardiovascular collapse, especially just after intubation and positive-pressure ventilation (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Increase to 100% oxygen at high flow and hand-ventilate to gauge compliance.
  2. Turn off nitrous oxide immediately so it cannot expand the trapped air. (adjunct — not on the Stanford aid)
  3. Call for help, call for the code cart, and inform the team; ask the surgeon to pause.
  4. Rule out a mainstem (endobronchial) intubation as the cause of the findings.
  5. Consider bedside ultrasound or a stat chest X-ray, but do not delay treatment if the patient is hemodynamically unstable.
  6. Place a 14- or 16-gauge needle in the 2nd intercostal space at the midclavicular line on the affected side; you may hear a whoosh of air if the pneumothorax is under tension.
  7. Alternatively, decompress at the 4th/5th intercostal space in the anterior-axillary line, which may better reach a thick chest wall. (adjunct — not on the Stanford aid)
  8. Immediately follow needle decompression with a thoracostomy (chest tube) for definitive drainage.
  9. Support hemodynamics with fluids and a vasopressor while decompressing. (adjunct — not on the Stanford aid)

Operating Room Power Failure

Loss of electrical power to the OR that threatens ventilation, monitoring, and anesthetic delivery · blackout · electrical failure

Premium
When to suspect
  • Room lights, screens, and the surgical field go dark at once
  • Anesthesia machine drops to battery mode with an audible alarm and countdown
  • Ventilator stops cycling or the vaporizer output becomes unreliable
  • Infusion pumps alarm and stall, interrupting TIVA and pressors
  • Monitors, warmers, and electrocautery lose power or run on backup only
  • Loss of oxygen pipeline pressure if a power event also hits the gas plant (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Get light immediately: grab additional light sources—lit laryngoscope handles, cell-phone flashlights, and portable flashlights for the field and airway.
  2. Open doors and shades to let ambient room light in.
  3. Confirm the ventilator is still cycling; if it is not, switch to hand ventilation with a self-inflating (bag-valve) resuscitator and convert to TIVA.
  4. Maintain anesthesia intravenously (TIVA), since vaporizers and infusion pumps may be lost—give incremental IV boluses to keep the patient anesthetized.
  5. If monitors fail, fall back to a manual check—palpate a pulse and take a manual blood pressure.
  6. Request a transport monitor or a defibrillator monitor to restore continuous monitoring.
  7. Confirm an adequate backup oxygen supply; a power event can affect gas supply or alarms, so verify O2 and keep a backup cylinder ready.
  8. Determine the extent of the failure: call biomedical/engineering and ask whether it is one OR, all ORs, or hospital-wide; if it is isolated to your room, check whether a circuit breaker has tripped.
  9. Do not start new cases; pause elective steps, control bleeding, and reach a safe stopping point.
  10. (adjunct — not on the Stanford aid) Move life-sustaining equipment to red emergency (generator-backed) outlets; generator power typically restores within about 10 seconds.

Pitfalls
  • Not knowing your machine's battery runtime—reserve is minutes, not hours, and shorter under full ventilator load.
  • Assuming loss of power means loss of oxygen; pipeline gas is pneumatic and usually keeps flowing.
  • Letting awareness happen when the vaporizer or TIVA pump silently stops delivering.
  • Plugging critical equipment into ordinary (non-red) outlets that are not on the generator.
  • Forgetting the vaporizer needs gas flow: with the ventilator off, agent delivery is unreliable.
  • Starting or continuing an elective case instead of stabilizing and pausing.
After the crisis

Once power is restored, reconnect equipment to normal outlets, re-verify the anesthesia machine and monitors, and recharge machine and pump batteries before the next case. Reassess depth of anesthesia and hemodynamics, and check that IV agents given by hand kept the patient adequately anesthetized. Document the outage timeline, what ran on battery versus generator, and any gaps in ventilation, monitoring, or delivery. Report the event through your safety system and debrief with the OR and facilities teams; confirm generator and battery maintenance. Know your institution's power-failure plan in advance. Total (High) Spinal Anesthesia

Excessively cephalad neuraxial block reaching cervical roots and the brainstem, causing cardiorespiratory collapse and loss of consciousness · high spinal, total spinal block

Premium
When to suspect
  • Block climbing higher or faster than expected after a spinal, epidural, or combined technique — especially an epidural dose given through a catheter after a known or suspected wet tap (accidental dural puncture)
  • Nausea, restlessness, or a vague "I don't feel right" — often the earliest warning, just before the pressure drops
  • Numbness, tingling, or weakness spreading into the hands and arms (cervical roots involved)
  • Dyspnea, a whispering or fading voice, and difficulty swallowing as intercostal and phrenic function fail
  • Hypotension and bradycardia from a high sympathetic block (cardioaccelerators at T1–T4)
  • Apnea, loss of consciousness, and fixed, dilated pupils from brainstem anesthesia (late)
  • Bradyasystolic cardiac arrest if unrecognized (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help, declare an emergency, and get the code cart and airway equipment to the room.
  2. Stop any ongoing epidural or intrathecal injection.
  3. Give 100% oxygen at 10–15 L/min and support ventilation; be ready to bag-mask and intubate, as respiratory failure can last several hours until the block recedes.
  4. Check for a pulse — if absent, begin CPR and follow the arrest algorithm (bradyasystolic collapse is typical).
  5. Treat severe bradycardia or hypotension aggressively and early with epinephrine; do not wait for arrest.
  6. For milder bradycardia, give atropine, but escalate quickly to epinephrine if it does not respond (glycopyrrolate is a reasonable off-aid alternative).
  7. Open IV fluids wide with a pressure bag and raise both legs to boost preload; keep the patient roughly neutral rather than steeply head-down, which drives the block higher.
  8. If the patient is still awake, reassure them and give a small amnestic (e.g., midazolam) to prevent awareness during ventilation.
  9. If obstetric: maintain left uterine displacement, call OB and neonatology, monitor fetal heart tones, and prepare for emergent or perimortem cesarean delivery.

Pitfalls
  • Redosing an epidural after a wet tap is a classic setup — local anesthetic tracks intrathecally through the dural hole, so re-dose in small increments and watch the level closely.
  • Do not wait for arrest — ascending arm weakness, a fading voice, or dyspnea means intervene now, before apnea.
  • Phenylephrine alone often fails — the block is bradycardic, and a pure alpha-agonist can deepen the reflex slowing; reach for ephedrine or epinephrine.
  • Avoid steep Trendelenburg to chase pressure — head-down helps venous return but pushes the block even higher; use leg raise and fluids instead.
  • An awake but paralyzed patient can be aware — secure ventilation and give an amnestic, do not assume unconsciousness.
  • Plan for hours, not minutes — keep the airway and hemodynamics supported until the block regresses; premature extubation risks re-arrest.
  • Consider local anesthetic systemic toxicity if the picture fits — if intravascular injection is possible, treat with 20% lipid emulsion.
After the crisis

Continue full airway and hemodynamic support in a monitored setting (typically PACU or ICU) until the block clearly recedes and spontaneous ventilation, airway reflexes, and hand strength return; this often takes several hours. Extubate only once the level has regressed and the patient can protect the airway and breathe adequately. Debrief the patient about intraoperative events, document the block level, drug, and timeline, and complete institutional incident reporting. Review the neuraxial technique and dosing with the team, and flag any prior wet tap in the record. Management above is cross-checked against the Stanford Emergency Manual.

Transfusion Reaction

An adverse response to a transfused blood product, ranging from a benign fever to fatal haemolysis, lung injury, or anaphylaxis — the first move is always to stop the unit and identify the type · acute haemolytic reaction, TRALI, TACO, febrile non-haemolytic reaction, allergic / anaphylactic reaction, septic transfusion reaction

Premium
When to suspect
  • Any new fever, chills, or rigors during or shortly after a unit is started — the most common early sign, though it never rules out a dangerous reaction on its own
  • Fever with back or flank pain, dark or red urine (haemoglobinuria), and hypotension → acute haemolytic reaction, usually from an ABO / clerical mismatch and often within minutes of starting the unit
  • Under anaesthesia the usual symptoms are masked — unexplained hypotension, tachycardia, haemoglobinuria in the Foley, diffuse oozing, or a rising temperature may be the only clues
  • New hypoxaemia with bilateral pulmonary infiltrates within 6 hours of transfusion, without volume overload → TRALI
  • Dyspnoea with hypertension, raised JVP, crackles, and rising airway pressures within ~6–12 hours → TACO (circulatory overload)
  • Urticaria and itching alone suggest a mild allergic reaction; add hypotension, wheeze, or angioedema and treat as anaphylaxis
  • High fever with rigors, hypotension, and shock soon after starting a unit (platelets are the highest-risk product) → bacterial contamination / sepsis (adjunct — not a named reaction type on the Stanford aid, which lists only haemolytic, febrile, and anaphylactic)
  • Diffuse microvascular bleeding or oozing from the field and line sites = haemolysis with DIC (late)
  • Jaundice with an unexplained haemoglobin drop days to weeks later = delayed haemolytic reaction (not an intra-op emergency, but flag it) (late)
Immediate actions
  1. STOP the transfusion immediately at the first sign of a reaction — this is the single most important action and comes before any workup.
  2. Keep the IV line open with 0.9% saline through fresh tubing; do not flush the residual blood in the existing line into the patient.
  3. Recheck the patient’s identity against the unit — compare the wristband, the unit label, and the paperwork side by side to catch a clerical / ABO mismatch.
  4. Call for help, tell the surgeon, and get the code cart; assign a leader and consider pausing the procedure.
  5. Give 100% oxygen and support the airway and breathing; intubate for airway compromise or severe hypoxaemia if not already tubed.
  6. Support the circulation — if hypotensive, turn the anaesthetic down, give an IV crystalloid bolus, and treat with a vasopressor (phenylephrine or ephedrine); for severe hypotension give epinephrine and/or vasopressin.
  7. Identify the reaction type from the pattern (haemolytic vs TRALI vs TACO vs febrile vs allergic / anaphylactic vs septic) and treat by type — the specific therapies differ.
  8. Acute haemolytic: aggressively hydrate and maintain urine output ~1 mL/kg/hr with fluids, a diuretic, and (per the Stanford aid) renal-dose dopamine if needed; monitor for and treat DIC; consider alkalinising the urine with sodium bicarbonate (adjunct — not on the Stanford aid).
  9. TRALI: supportive respiratory care and lung-protective ventilation; TACO: slow / stop volume, sit the patient up, diurese, and give oxygen.
  10. Anaphylaxis: epinephrine early (bolus then infusion) with fluids; septic reaction: draw cultures and start broad-spectrum antibiotics with aggressive resuscitation.
  11. Return the unit, bag, and tubing to the blood bank and send post-transfusion samples (repeat type & crossmatch, direct antiglobulin / Coombs, haemolysis labs, and a fresh urine sample) per local protocol.
  12. Consult the transfusion medicine / blood-bank physician for guidance on the workup and on holding or reissuing further products.

Pitfalls
  • Not stopping the unit fast enough — the volume of incompatible blood already infused drives severity; stop first, investigate second.
  • Flushing the line — pushing the blood remaining in the tubing into the patient delivers more of the offending product; switch to saline through fresh tubing.
  • Skipping the clerical / ID recheck — most fatal acute haemolytic reactions are ABO mismatches from a bedside identification or labelling error, which the recheck catches immediately.
  • Assuming every fever is benign — a febrile reaction is a diagnosis of exclusion; rule out haemolysis and bacterial contamination before calling it non-haemolytic.
  • Confusing TRALI with TACO — both cause hypoxaemia and infiltrates, but TACO is volume overload (hypertension, raised JVP → diurese) while TRALI is not (do not diurese; give respiratory support).
  • Missing haemolysis under anaesthesia — with symptoms masked, haemoglobinuria in the Foley, unexplained hypotension, or diffuse oozing may be the only signs; keep it in the differential.
  • Under-treating anaphylaxis with antihistamines and steroids — epinephrine and fluids are the life-saving therapies, not the adjuncts.
  • Forgetting sepsis — platelets are stored at room temperature and carry the highest contamination risk; high fever with shock needs cultures and antibiotics, not just an antipyretic.
  • Not returning the unit or sending the right samples — the blood-bank workup depends on the bag, tubing, and correctly timed post-transfusion specimens.
After the crisis

Disposition follows severity: a mild febrile or urticarial reaction may only need observation, while acute haemolysis, TRALI, anaphylaxis, or a septic reaction warrants ICU-level monitoring. Continue to track vital signs, urine output, oxygenation, and coagulation, and support the kidneys, lungs, and circulation as dictated by the reaction type. Complete the blood-bank workup — return the unit, bag, and tubing and send the post-transfusion samples (repeat type & crossmatch, direct antiglobulin / Coombs, haemolysis markers such as LDH, haptoglobin, bilirubin and plasma free haemoglobin, and a urine sample), and hold or reissue products per their guidance. Document the timeline, the product and unit numbers, the presenting signs, and every treatment, and file the mandatory transfusion-reaction report with the blood bank / transfusion service; for a suspected septic reaction, culture both the patient and the unit and notify the supplier. In the US, serious transfusion-related events are reportable to the FDA (and, for a transfusion-related fatality, to the FDA within the required timeframe). Update the chart and inform the patient and team of the reaction and any implications for future transfusion. Venous Air Embolism

Air drawn into an open vein above heart level, obstructing the right heart and pulmonary outflow · VAE · air lock

Premium
When to suspect
  • High-risk setup: sitting or semi-sitting craniotomy, posterior fossa or cervical spine, laparoscopy under insufflation, or any wound above the heart
  • Open central-line placement, an unprimed line, or a disconnected hub with the vein above heart level
  • Air seen on TEE or a change in the precordial Doppler tone (the most sensitive practical monitors)
  • Abrupt fall in ETCO2 as embolized air enlarges the dead space
  • Falling blood pressure with tachycardia and dysrhythmia
  • Falling SpO2
  • Rising central venous pressure
  • New dyspnea, respiratory distress, or cough in an awake patient
  • A churning, machinery-like "mill-wheel" murmur over the precordium (late)
Immediate actions
  1. Call for help and the code cart, and inform the team.
  2. Raise inspired oxygen to 100% at high flow.
  3. Tell the surgeon to flood the surgical field with saline to stop further entrainment.
  4. Lower the surgical site below the level of the heart when able, to raise venous pressure at the wound.
  5. If a central line is in place, aspirate to withdraw air from the right heart.
  6. Give a rapid fluid bolus to raise CVP and oppose further air entry.
  7. Turn the volatile agent down or off.
  8. Give epinephrine, starting 10–100 mcg IV and titrating, to maintain cardiac output.
  9. If blood pressure is catastrophically low, start CPR; compressions may also fragment and disperse the air lock.
  10. Consider TTE or TEE echocardiography to assess the air burden and right-ventricular function.
  11. Consider repositioning left-lateral decubitus.
  12. If the situation is severe, terminate the procedure when able.
  13. Adjunct — not on the Stanford aid: stop nitrous oxide, which diffuses into bubbles and enlarges the air lock; a left-lateral head-down (Durant) tilt to trap air at the RV apex; and surgeon-applied bilateral jugular compression to reveal a cranial entry point.

Pitfalls
  • Waiting for the mill-wheel murmur—it is a late sign; the ETCO2 drop and Doppler change come first.
  • Not telling the surgeon immediately to flood the field, the most direct way to stop entrainment.
  • Forgetting to lower the surgical site below the heart when able.
  • Under-dosing epinephrine—Stanford starts at 10–100 mcg to maintain cardiac output.
  • Overlooking a paradoxical (arterial) embolus with a patent foramen ovale, causing stroke or coronary ischemia.
  • Letting catheter aspiration delay chest compressions and epinephrine when the patient is collapsing.
  • Missing air pulled in through an open, unprimed, or disconnected IV or central line rather than the wound.
After the crisis

Keep FiO2 high and monitor closely until ETCO2, blood pressure, and saturation stabilize; expect a lag as the air resolves. Weigh a formal search for a right-to-left shunt (patent foramen ovale). Send an arterial gas, watch for pulmonary edema and arrhythmia, and get imaging as indicated. Beyond the Stanford aid, if paradoxical embolism or a new neurologic deficit is suspected, consult for early hyperbaric oxygen. For future high-risk cases, revisit monitoring and positioning and confirm every line is primed and air-free. Document the trigger, air source, detection, and response; debrief the team and file the event report. Scope and values cross-checked against the Stanford Emergency Manual, event #25.
