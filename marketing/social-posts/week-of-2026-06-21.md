🧠 Sunday board-review check

A patient receives an interscalene brachial plexus block for shoulder surgery and soon reports mild shortness of breath. What's the most likely explanation?

A. Pneumothorax
B. Ipsilateral hemidiaphragmatic paresis (phrenic nerve block)
C. Local anesthetic systemic toxicity
D. Bronchospasm

Answer: B. The phrenic nerve (C3–C5) lies just anterior to the interscalene groove, so interscalene blocks cause ipsilateral hemidiaphragmatic paresis in close to 100% of cases at traditional volumes — usually well tolerated, but a reason for caution in patients with limited pulmonary reserve.

## MON, Jun 22 — Question of the Day (Pharmacology)

### Company LinkedIn
🧠 MACPrep Question of the Day

A patient under general anesthesia received high-dose rocuronium and now needs immediate reversal of a deep neuromuscular block. Which agent is the best choice?

A. Neostigmine
B. Sugammadex
C. Edrophonium
D. Pyridostigmine

Answer: B — Sugammadex. It's a modified γ-cyclodextrin that directly encapsulates aminosteroid agents (rocuronium > vecuronium), so it can rapidly reverse even a deep block — something acetylcholinesterase inhibitors like neostigmine cannot reliably do at deep levels.
